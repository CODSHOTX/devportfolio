A) Iot infrastructure
A typical Internet of Things (IoT) infrastructure for greenhouses would likely include a variety of sensors and actuators connected to a central hub or gateway.
  • The sensors might measure various environmental conditions such as temperature, humidity, light levels, soil moisture, and CO2 levels. These sensors would be connected to the central hub or gateway, which would then transmit the data to a cloud-based platform for storage and analysis.

  • Actuators , such as fans, heaters, and irrigation systems, would also be connected to the central hub or gateway. These devices would be controlled by the central hub or gateway based on the data received from the sensors and the instructions given by the cloud-based platform.

  • The cloud-based platform would be used to analyze the data collected by the sensors and make decisions about how to control the actuators. For example, if the temperature in the greenhouse is too high, the cloud-based platform might instruct the central hub or gateway to turn on the fans.

  • Mobile Application (Apps) available for the farmer/grower, who can monitor the conditions of their greenhouse and make necessary adjustments from anywhere at any time. The mobile app will be connected to the cloud and all the data will be synced for viewing and control. Overall, a typical IoT infrastructure for a greenhouse would include a variety of sensors and actuators connected to a central hub or gateway, which transmits data to a cloud-based platform for analysis and control.
B) Constraints
There are a number of constraints that you may encounter when operating a greenhouse. Some of the most common include:
  • Environmental conditions: Greenhouses rely on a delicate balance of temperature, humidity, light, and other environmental factors to grow plants successfully. High temperatures, low humidity, and too much or too little light can all negatively impact plant growth and development.

  • Water and nutrient management: Greenhouses require a consistent and reliable source of water and nutrients in order to support plant growth. Insufficient water or nutrient availability can cause stunted growth, wilting, and even plant death.

  • Pest and disease management: Greenhouses are often subject to infestations of pests and diseases, which can quickly spread and cause significant damage to crops. Proper pest and disease management is essential to prevent or minimize damage.

  • Lighting: Greenhouses rely on artificial lighting to supplement natural light, especially during winter or cloudy days. This can be quite expensive, and the energy costs can be one of the largest costs associated with running a greenhouse.

  • Space and ventilation: Greenhouses are typically limited in terms of the amount of space available, which can make it difficult to grow a wide variety of plants. Proper ventilation is also crucial to control the temperature and humidity and prevent the buildup of harmful gases.

  • Financial: Greenhouse operation and maintenance can be a significant financial investment. The costs of constructing, heating, and maintaining a greenhouse can be high, and it's important to carefully consider the potential return on investment before committing to a greenhouse operation.
